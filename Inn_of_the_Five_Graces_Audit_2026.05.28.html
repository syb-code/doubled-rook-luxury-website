Executive Summary
A world-class property with avoidable technical debt undermining its digital presence

The Inn of the Five Graces is a genuinely distinguished property — a Relais & Châteaux member, ranked #1 of 60 Santa Fe hotels on TripAdvisor, recognized by Travel + Leisure as a World's Best Hotel, and a 2026 Condé Nast Traveler nominee. Its physical offering is exceptional: centuries-old adobe architecture, hand-crafted interiors, Moroccan tilework, and a spa and dining program that draws guests from across the country. The digital infrastructure surrounding this property does not yet reflect that standing.

This audit identifies 19 findings across four domains: website accessibility and technical hygiene, marketing measurement and SEO, Google and OTA distribution, and social media. Three are scanner-visible WCAG Level A failures — a missing H1 element, eight images with empty alt text (including the brand logo), and unnamed social media links — all remediation-simple. Separately, the property fails Google's Core Web Vitals assessment on both mobile and desktop, driven by a server response time (TTFB) of roughly three seconds, and three tag containers fire simultaneously (two GTM containers plus a direct GA4 load), creating potential measurement duplication that can corrupt attribution data if not configured correctly.

The hotel's structured data implementation is a genuine asset: Hotel schema with geo coordinates, star rating, aggregate review data, and nested spa sub-entity is well above the industry baseline. TripAdvisor position and OTA distribution through Booking.com, Expedia, KAYAK, and American Express Fine Hotels + Resorts provide strong organic placement. These foundations are sound; the gaps are concentrated in accessibility compliance, site performance, tag governance, and one significant content distribution channel — TikTok — which the property's visual identity is exceptionally well-positioned to exploit.

The remediation roadmap that follows is sequenced across four phases: defusing immediate compliance risk in Week 1, stabilizing measurement, performance, and distribution foundations through Week 4, launching new content and social channels through Week 10, and establishing a sustained optimization cycle from Month 3 forward. None of the Phase 1 or Phase 2 items require design changes or new content — they are purely technical corrections that a developer can action in a single sprint.

Chapter 01

Website & Accessibility

Eight findings spanning WCAG conformance, heading structure, image alt text, link labeling, video description, tag hygiene, and structured data — including three Critical Level A failures and one standout positive.

F-01
Critical WCAG 1.3.1 · Level A · Info and Relationships
No H1 element exists anywhere on the homepage
The homepage uses <h2> and <h3> elements throughout but contains no <h1>. Screen readers announce page structure based on heading hierarchy; without an H1, assistive technology users have no primary landmark to orient from. Search engines also use the H1 as the primary on-page relevance signal for keyword targeting and document outline interpretation. The first two headings on the page are visually-hidden menu labels (<h2>Primary Menu</h2> and <h2>Seconday Menu</h2> — note the property's own typo), after which the document jumps directly to <h3> for visible content, skipping the top level of the outline entirely. This is a scanner-visible Level A failure and one of the most straightforward heading-structure violations to remediate.
grep -c "<h1" fivegraces.com.html → 0 results Heading order detected (homepage): h2 → h2 → h3 → h3 → h3 → h3 → h3 → h3 ... First headings in document (both visually hidden menu labels): <h2 class="screen-reader-text">Primary Menu</h2> <h2 class="screen-reader-text">Seconday Menu</h2> Expected: h1 (visible primary headline) → h2 → h3
F-02
Critical WCAG 1.1.1 · Level A · Non-text Content
Eight images carry empty alt attributes — including the brand logo
Of 50 images on the homepage, eight are coded with alt="". Empty alt is the correct pattern for purely decorative graphics, but several of these carry meaningful content. The most significant is the brand logo mark (fg-mark-4.png) — empty alt on a logo is the single most-cited Title-level violation pattern, because the property's own name is invisible to a screen reader at the top of the page. The dining and features images (Pink Adobe atmosphere photography, the property dining pair, and an above-the-fold intro graphic) are also content-bearing and should be described. To a screen reader, none of these exist; to an automated WCAG scanner, the logo case in particular is sourced in seconds.
8 of 50 <img> tags carry alt="" (confirmed in page source): fg-mark-4.png alt="" ← brand logo mark 5graces_dining4_sm.jpg alt="" 5graces_dining4_lg.jpg alt="" Talitha-Tarro-Photography-Pink-Adobe_13 alt="" bytqw4el.png alt="" yxxfcnrz.png alt="" 953bk33m.png alt="" ixv1o3cr.png alt="" Fix: add descriptive alt to the logo ("The Inn of the Five Graces") and to each content image; retain alt="" only for genuinely decorative graphics.
F-03
Critical WCAG 2.4.4 · Level A · Link Purpose
Footer social media links have no accessible name
The Facebook and Instagram footer links consist of icon-only <a> elements containing Font Awesome <i> icons with a title attribute on the child element. The title on the <i> child does not propagate as an accessible name for the parent <a> — screen readers will announce these as "link" with no destination or purpose whatsoever. An aria-label attribute on each anchor element is required to satisfy WCAG 2.4.4 Level A. This is a two-line fix.
<a target="_blank" href="https://www.facebook.com/TheFiveGraces"> <i class="fa-brands fa-facebook-f" title="Facebook"></i> </a> <a target="_blank" href="https://www.instagram.com/thefivegraces/"> <i class="fa-brands fa-instagram" title="Instagram"></i> </a> Confirmed: 0 aria-label attributes on either social anchor. Fix: add aria-label="Visit us on Facebook" and aria-label="Visit us on Instagram" to each respective <a> element.
F-04
Minor Tag Hygiene · Dual GTM Containers
Two GTM containers fire simultaneously, creating duplicate dataLayer event risk
The site loads two separate Google Tag Manager containers: GTM-P6PVWMV3 (property-managed) and GTM-5SQ2GHZ (Vizergy's booking engine container). Both push to the same window.dataLayer array. If any tags — GA4, conversion pixels, remarketing audiences — are configured in both containers, event counts will be artificially doubled, corrupting attribution data and inflating conversion reporting. This is particularly consequential for booking conversion tracking where double-counting can misrepresent revenue attribution.
(window,document,'script','dataLayer','GTM-P6PVWMV3') // property GTM (window,document,'script','dataLayer','GTM-5SQ2GHZ') // Vizergy GTM Both present in <head>. Both push to window.dataLayer. Risk: any tag duplicated across both containers fires twice per event. Action: audit both GTM workspaces; remove redundant tag configurations.
F-05
Positive Structured Data · Hotel Schema
Hotel structured data is comprehensive and well-formed
A custom Hotel schema block provides geo coordinates, star rating (5), aggregateRating (4.8 / 164 reviews), priceRange (min $600 / max $2500), address, telephone, and a containsPlace entry for The Spa of The Five Graces with its own operating hours and contact data. This implementation is well above the industry baseline and gives Google rich data to surface in hotel knowledge panels, price calendars, and AI-generated travel summaries. The nested spa sub-entity is particularly well-executed and should be used as a model for future room-level schema additions. (Note: the on-site schema's 4.8 aggregateRating reflects the property's own review set and differs from the 4.0 bubble rating displayed on TripAdvisor in F-17 — the two are sourced independently and are not in conflict.)

Chapter 02

Marketing & Measurement

Seven findings covering measurement redundancy, FAQ schema, a failed Core Web Vitals assessment, front-end performance, on-page SEO, social-sharing metadata, and TikTok distribution — with clear remediation paths for each.

F-06
Moderate SEO · FAQPage Schema · AEO
FAQ page exists but carries no FAQPage structured data
The site links to /frequently-asked-questions/ from the footer navigation, but no FAQPage schema block was found on the homepage and the FAQ page itself could not be confirmed to carry structured data during this audit run. FAQPage markup enables FAQ rich results in Google Search — collapsed Q&A directly in the SERP beneath the organic listing — which is one of the highest-visibility AEO (Answer Engine Optimization) placements available to hotels without any paid spend. For luxury properties fielding questions about check-in, pets, cancellation policy, and dining reservations, this is a direct conversion-support opportunity.
Footer nav confirmed: <a href="https://fivegraces.com/frequently-asked-questions/">FAQs</a> No FAQPage schema found in homepage source: grep '"@type":"FAQPage"' fivegraces.com.html → 0 results grep '"@type":"Question"' fivegraces.com.html → 0 results Action: add FAQPage schema to /frequently-asked-questions/ with top 8–12 Q&A pairs covering check-in, dining, spa, pet policy, cancellation, parking.
F-07
Moderate Performance · Core Web Vitals · Confirmed Ranking Factor
Core Web Vitals fail Google's threshold on both mobile and desktop
Live PageSpeed Insights field data (28-day Chrome User Experience Report ending May 28, 2026) returns a failed Core Web Vitals assessment on both mobile and desktop. The assessment fails on a single metric — Largest Contentful Paint — while Interaction to Next Paint and Cumulative Layout Shift both pass comfortably. LCP here is a symptom rather than the disease: the underlying cause is a Time to First Byte of 3.1 seconds on mobile and 2.7 seconds on desktop, meaning the server spends roughly three seconds generating the page before any content can render. First Contentful Paint (3.7s mobile / 3.3s desktop) inherits the same delay. Page speed is a confirmed Google ranking factor, and the gap between a 3.4–3.8 second LCP and the 2.5-second target translates directly to lost organic visibility and measurable mobile conversion loss. The root-cause front-end contributors are documented in F-12. Direct report: pagespeed.web.dev/analysis/https-fivegraces-com.

F-08
Moderate Performance · Front-End Root Causes
Slow server response, no lazy loading, render-blocking CSS, and legacy image formats
A source-level scan of the homepage identifies the structural causes behind the failed Core Web Vitals score in F-11. The dominant lever is server response time: a direct fetch returned the first byte in roughly two seconds, consistent with the three-second field TTFB. The site is WordPress on Apache behind Cloudflare, but no page-caching or optimization plugin signature was detected, which is the typical cause of a multi-second TTFB on this stack. On the front end, none of the 50 homepage images use loading="lazy", so every image loads eagerly regardless of whether it is in the viewport; 21 render-blocking stylesheets sit in the <head>, inflating First Contentful Paint; and zero content images use modern formats (WebP or AVIF) — they are served as legacy JPG and PNG. There are also no preconnect or preload hints to prioritize the LCP image. In priority order, the highest-ROI fixes are: cut TTFB with proper full-page caching, then lazy-load below-the-fold images, then convert imagery to WebP, then trim the stylesheet count.

F-09
Minor Social Sharing · Open Graph Image Missing
No og:image or twitter:image — shared links render with no preview
The page implements most Open Graph tags (og:title, og:description, og:url, og:type, og:locale) and a twitter:card tag, but specifies no og:image and no twitter:image. Every time the URL is shared — in iMessage, Slack, WhatsApp, Facebook, or LinkedIn — the link unfurls as a bare headline with no photograph. For a property whose entire commercial case is visual luxury, a missing share image is a brand leak on every link a guest, partner, or press contact pastes. Adding a single high-quality hero image reference is a one-line fix per tag.
Open Graph tags present: og:title, og:description, og:url, og:type, og:locale Missing: og:image, twitter:image twitter:card present but no paired image specified. Result: shared links unfurl with no preview image across all platforms. Fix: add <meta property="og:image" content=".../hero.jpg"> and a matching <meta name="twitter:image" content=".../hero.jpg"> (1200×630 recommended).

Chapter 05

Remediation Roadmap

Four sequenced phases: defuse immediate compliance risk first, stabilize measurement and performance, launch new content channels, then establish a sustained optimization cycle.

Priority Note
Phase 1 contains three WCAG Level A failures. These are the highest-priority items in this audit — each can be corrected in under 30 minutes of development time and should be actioned before any other work begins. F-01, F-02, and F-03 together constitute the core of the accessibility exposure. The server-response work behind the failed Core Web Vitals score (F-11, F-12) is the highest-impact revenue lever and is scheduled in Phase 2.
Phase 1
Week 1 — Defuse Risk & Quick Wins
Immediate Compliance Corrections
  • Add <h1> to the homepage — make the primary hero headline an H1 element, not H3. Do not use screen-reader-only or visually-hidden technique; the H1 should be the visible primary headline of the page. Addresses F-01.
  • Add aria-label to social link anchors — add aria-label="Visit us on Facebook" and aria-label="Visit us on Instagram" to the respective footer <a> elements. Two-line fix. Addresses F-03.
  • Add descriptive alt text to all eight empty-alt images — including the brand logo (alt="The Inn of the Five Graces") and each dining/feature image. Example: alt="Interior dining at the Pink Adobe with warm adobe walls and candlelit tables". Addresses F-02.
  • Add og:image and twitter:image — reference a 1200×630 hero photo so shared links unfurl with a preview image across all platforms. One line per tag. Addresses F-14.
  • Tighten the homepage title tag — lead with the brand and a single strongest qualifier, attribute or remove the unqualified "#1" claim, and bring length under the SERP truncation point. Addresses F-13.
Phase 2
Weeks 2–4 — Measurement, Performance & Distribution Foundations
Tag Governance, Site Speed, Video, Accessibility Statement, Distribution Verification
  • Cut TTFB and clear the Core Web Vitals failure — enable proper full-page caching at origin, confirm Cloudflare is serving cached HTML, and verify the hosting plan is not the constraint; then lazy-load below-the-fold images, convert imagery to WebP, and reduce the render-blocking stylesheet count. Target mobile LCP under 2.5s. Addresses F-11 and F-12.
  • Consolidate three tag loads into a single GTM container — audit GTM-P6PVWMV3 and GTM-5SQ2GHZ for overlapping tags; remove the direct GA4 script load; confirm GA4 G-1TYCSHFLQV fires only once per pageview. Addresses F-06 and F-09.
  • Add <track kind="descriptions"> to the spa section video — author a WebVTT description track describing the spa visual sequence; attach as a child element of the <video> tag. Addresses F-05.
  • Publish a formal accessibility statement at /accessibility/ — document the WCAG target (2.1 AA), list known gaps, and provide a contact method for accommodation requests. Link from footer alongside Privacy Policy. Addresses F-04.
  • Add FAQPage schema markup to /frequently-asked-questions/ — implement JSON-LD FAQPage structured data covering top 8–12 questions. Validate in Google Rich Results Test. Addresses F-10.
Phase 3
Weeks 4–10 — Direct Booking, Content & Social Engine
TikTok Launch, Accessibility Sweep, Schema Expansion
  • Launch TikTok account @thefivegraces — establish account; develop 8-video content backlog before first post; publish at 3–4 videos/week cadence using existing photography and videography assets. Target content pillars: suite reveals, spa walkthroughs, Santa Fe POV, artisan detail close-ups. Addresses F-15 and F-18.
  • Add aria-label to all remaining icon-only links throughout the site — conduct full sweep of <a> elements containing only icon fonts or SVGs with no visible text; add descriptive aria-label to each. Expands F-03 fix site-wide.
  • Audit inner page heading hierarchy — confirm H1 presence and logical H2/H3 cascade on accommodations, spa, and dining section pages. Fix any heading-skip violations found. Extends F-01 fix across the domain.
  • Leverage Reels for full screen experience and cross post to TikTok to inspire younger demographics.
  • Re-test Core Web Vitals post-remediation — confirm the Phase 2 caching and image work has moved mobile and desktop into a passing assessment; document before/after LCP and TTFB. Verifies F-11 and F-12.
Phase 4
Month 3+ — Sustained Optimization
Schema Depth, LLM Content, Niche Landing Pages & UGC
  • Add containsPlace room-level schema for individual suites — implement LodgingBusiness sub-entity schema for named suites (Luminaria Villa, Adobe Rose, and others) with room features, max occupancy, and image references. Positions property for Google room-type rich results.
  • Build LLM-optimized FAQ content blocks — develop structured Q&A content targeting AI Overviews and Perplexity queries for luxury Santa Fe hotel searches; embed in FAQ and landing pages using visible text plus FAQ schema. Target queries: "best hotel in Santa Fe," "Relais & Châteaux New Mexico," "luxury spa hotel Santa Fe."
  • Develop niche landing pages — create dedicated pages for weddings, spa retreat packages, art residency / gallery season stays, and high-desert wellness travel. Each page to carry Event or Service schema where applicable.
  • Establish structured UGC re-post cadence — develop a repeatable workflow for identifying, licensing, and re-posting guest-generated content on Instagram and seeded TikTok; target 1–2 UGC reposts per week per platform to supplement original content output.
Precise diagnosis. Measured improvement.

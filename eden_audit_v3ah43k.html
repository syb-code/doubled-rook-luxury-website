Executive Summary

A property worth its rate that is being undersold by every channel that represents it online.

The Eden has the property to compete in Santa Monica's premium boutique tier — Streamline Moderne provenance, ocean-view rooms across 1301 Ocean Avenue, a real F&B program with Velvet Rose and Coffee Manufactory, and a guest experience that earns 8.0–8.2 ratings on the major OTAs. None of the marketing surfaces around it tell that story consistently, and several actively hurt the property today.

The website has compliance defects that put a California property in the standard target profile for ADA Title III litigation — a profile sourced by automated scanners, with active exposure under the Unruh Act's $4,000-per-violation regime. The exposure is not pending; it is active from the moment the defects exist. Separately, the site is operating without a functioning analytics or pixel layer (the plumbing is installed but never connected), and the booking architecture gives guests no reason to book direct.

Off the site, the official website does not appear on in Google Hotels Meta leaving OTAs uncontended. Brand ads do not appear to protect direct bookings. Lastly, Eden has a low listing ranking across OTAs.

The remediation path is sequenced and mostly inexpensive. The OTA cleanup and the legal-exposure fixes belong on the calendar this week.

F-01
Critical WCAG 1.1.1 · Level A · Non-text Content

Every image on the page has an empty alt attribute.

All eight images — the logo, the hero illustration, the dining photography, the contact-section banner — are coded with alt="". Empty alt is the correct pattern for purely decorative images, but it is being used here on the brand logo and on photographs that carry meaningful content. To a screen reader, none of these exist. To an automated WCAG scanner — and to the legal tooling plaintiff firms use to source cases — this is the single most cited Title III violation pattern.

8 of 8 <img> tags scanned · 0 with descriptive alt Logo, hero illustration, three dining hero photos, footer logo, contact banner, scroll button — all alt=""
F-02
Critical WCAG 1.3.1 / 2.4.6 · Level A / AA

No H1, and the heading hierarchy skips a level.

The page has zero <h1> elements. The first heading is an H2, followed by two more H2s, then a jump straight to H4 for the dining venue cards. Screen readers rely on heading order to build a navigable outline. Search engines also use heading hierarchy as a primary topical signal — this single fix doubles as an SEO win.

Detected order: h2 → h2 → h2 → h4 → h4 → h4 Skip detected: h2 → h4 (Coffee Manufactory, Poolside, Velvet Rose) Expected: h1 → h2 → h3
F-03
Critical ADA Title III · Safe-harbor signal

No accessibility statement is published or linked.

The footer references "Accessibility Notice" as plain text, but nothing links to it and no policy page exists at the property's domain. An accessibility statement is not, by itself, compliance — but its presence is one of the first things plaintiff attorneys check when sourcing defendants, and its absence removes a meaningful good-faith argument in any settlement conversation.

F-04
Major WCAG 4.1.2 · Level A · Name, Role, Value

Two empty links and two icon-only links lack accessible names.

Two <a> tags in the off-canvas mobile menu have no text, no aria-label, and no enclosed image — they are silent to assistive tech. Two more links wrap an image with empty alt and no aria-label (the home logo link and the scroll-to-intro chevron). Each renders as a focusable element a screen reader cannot announce.

F-05
Major WCAG 1.3.1 · Level A · Landmarks

Five duplicate <nav> landmarks; no <footer> element.

The page renders five <nav> elements — the same navigation duplicated for desktop, off-canvas mobile, and a sticky variant. Without distinct aria-labels, screen-reader users hear "navigation, navigation, navigation, navigation, navigation" and cannot tell which is which. The footer area uses generic <div> rather than a <footer> landmark.

F-06
Minor Brand consistency

Two different phone numbers represent the property across surfaces (Although, both phone lines work).

The footer displays 1 (310) 933 9113 while the underlying tel: link routes to 13103942791. Both numbers ring through to the property, so this is not a lost-call issue — but it is a brand and trust signal issue. A guest who notices the inconsistency loses a small amount of confidence in the property's attention to detail. More importantly, the 933-9113 number appears on Yelp and Tripadvisor while Visit Santa Monica's official tourism page publishes 310-394-2791 — meaning the older Sonder/Beacon-era line continues to propagate across third-party listings. This is a citation-consistency problem with downstream local-SEO consequences.

F-07
Minor Hero strategy

The hero says nothing about the rooms, Velvet Rose or Coffee Manufactory.

The homepage hero is the property's most expensive piece of real estate, and it is currently spending that real estate on ambient mood rather than on the property's actual revenue drivers. There is no hero treatment for the guest rooms, no hero for Coffee Manufactory (a genuine specialty draw and a partnership the property could lean into much harder), and no hero for events. A first-time visitor leaves the fold without a clear sense of what the property actually offers — which directly costs the booking funnel above the booking engine itself.

F-08
Minor Velvet Rose

Velvet Rose deserves its own hero treatment, not a card buried in a row.

Velvet Rose is a rooftop bar at a historic Streamline Moderne property in Santa Monica with vinyl-and-cocktails programming — a category-defining differentiator and a self-contained content engine. It is currently presented as one card in a row of three, sharing visual hierarchy with the coffee bar and the poolside food. The reference comparison is the Bower Coronado's Dive Rooftop page (bowercoronado.com/dive-rooftop/), which gives the rooftop venue its own dedicated page, hero, and narrative weight. Velvet Rose should have the same treatment — a dedicated landing page, an editorial hero, distinct photography, dedicated structured data.

F-09
Major Mobile experience

The mobile site looks dated and is not properly responsive.

Mobile rendering of the homepage shows undersized images and cards that do not scale appropriately to the viewport. Cards on the homepage in particular are too small at common mobile widths, forcing the hero and venue sections to feel cramped. For a boutique property targeting the kind of traveler who books from an iPhone in bed at 11pm, the mobile feel is a primary brand impression — and it is currently working against the property's positioning. Compounding this: the gallery on mobile is broken — only the first four images surface, and tapping into the gallery cycles between three to four photos rather than the full set.

F-10
Major Photography

Room photography in booking engine is not flattering and offers limited variation.

The room photography on the homepage and the rooms page does not flatter the property. The framing is tight, the lighting is mixed, and there are not enough angles per room type to give a guest a complete sense of the space, the bathroom, the view, or the design vocabulary. For a property whose differentiator is its 1939 Streamline Moderne architecture and ocean view, room imagery is doing the heaviest commercial work on the page — and is currently underdelivering. A dedicated photography refresh, scoped against OTA distribution requirements (40+ images per major listing, see Chapter 03) and the property's design language, is one of the highest-ROI investments available.

F-11
Major Performance · Core Web Vitals

Mobile Core Web Vitals fail Google's threshold

Live PageSpeed data on the rooms page (28-day Chrome User Experience Report, March 27 to April 23, 2026) returns a failed Core Web Vitals assessment on mobile. M-01
Critical Measurement

Analytics plumbing is installed but never connected — the property is running blind.

The story here is more interesting than "no analytics." GTM4WP and MonsterInsights are both installed. The site is actively pushing data into a dataLayer on every page load. Five lines later, the GTM4WP plugin itself prints the following to the browser console: "[GTM4WP] Google Tag Manager container code placement set to OFF !!! Data layer codes are active but GTM container must be loaded using custom coding." MonsterInsights is similarly explicit in source comments: "MonsterInsights is not currently configured on this site. The site owner needs to authenticate with Google Analytics." Somebody set this up. Nobody finished it. Every visit, every booking click, every dataLayer push is being thrown away.

var dataLayer = dataLayer || []; dataLayer.push( dataLayer_content ); console.warn("[GTM4WP] Google Tag Manager container code placement set to OFF !!!"); <!-- Note: MonsterInsights is not currently configured on this site. --> <!-- No tracking code set -->
M-02
Critical SEO · AEO · GEO

No meta description, no Hotel schema, weak title tag.

The homepage has no meta description. The title tag, "HOME - The Eden Hotel," wastes prime SERP real estate by leading with a generic word and — more importantly — by omitting the two words that should anchor every search impression for this property: boutique and Santa Monica. Schema markup includes WebPage, BreadcrumbList, and Organization — but the most important type for a hotel, LodgingBusiness with star rating, amenities, geo, and price range, is absent. This impacts both classical SEO and AI Overview / AEO eligibility on Google, Perplexity, ChatGPT, and Claude. Across the rest of the site, on-page SEO and GEO/AEO optimization is similarly underbuilt — meta tags, on-page copy, and structured data need a coordinated pass.

M-03
Major Performance · Core Web Vitals

25 external scripts, 29 stylesheets, 0 modern image formats.

Typical of an Elementor-built WordPress site that has accumulated plugins. Only 1 of 25 external scripts uses defer or async. None of the eight homepage images uses WebP or AVIF. Two of eight use loading="lazy". This profile is the structural cause of the failed mobile Core Web Vitals score documented in F-14 — the 3.3-second mobile LCP is being driven by the render-blocking script and stylesheet load, not by image weight alone.

M-04
Major Conversion · Direct booking

No direct-book incentive, no rate-parity messaging, no email capture above fold.

The booking engine link routes cleanly to Synxis — but nothing on the page tells a guest why they should book direct rather than on Expedia, Booking, or Hotels.com (where, as Chapter 03 will show, the brand identity itself is inconsistent). There is no best-rate language, no perk callout (welcome amenity, free parking, late checkout), no member rate, no loyalty offer. There is also no email capture mechanism near the hero. For an independent property without a major flag's loyalty machine, direct-book incentive is the most important commercial lever on the homepage.

M-05
Major Email capture

There is no email signup on every page — the property is losing leads to its own email marketing flows.

An email capture should appear on every meaningful page of the site: pre-footer at minimum, ideally also in the hero and at exit-intent on key pages. The current site has a Mailchimp form embed in one location and nothing else, meaning every visitor who is not ready to book is leaving without entering any nurture flow. For a property in Santa Monica's research-heavy travel-planning audience, this is one of the most expensive omissions on the site — every uncapture lead is a future booking the property will never know it had access to.

M-06
Major SEO · Keyword opportunity

The property is invisible for the high-volume Santa Monica hotel keywords.

If The Eden's site continues in its current state, it will not rank for the queries that matter most to the property's commercial future. Three keyword opportunities in particular stand out:

"hotels in santa monica" ~111,000 monthly searches "hotels near santa monica pier" ~6,600 monthly searches "boutique hotels santa monica" ~1,900 monthly searches

These are not aspirational keywords; they are the queries Eden's actual prospective guests are typing. Capturing even modest organic share of "hotels in santa monica" alone would represent meaningful incremental room nights per month. The current SEO posture — no meta descriptions, weak title tags, no LodgingBusiness schema, no FAQ markup, no neighborhood landing pages, no internal linking strategy, no AEO/GEO content cadence — leaves all of this volume on the table.

M-07
Major AEO · LLM optimization

No FAQ section anywhere on the site — a missed AEO foundation.

An FAQ section answers two strategic problems at once: it gives the guest fast access to the questions they actually have (parking, check-in time, pet policy, breakfast, walkability, ResortPass), and it gives AI answer engines (Google AI Overviews, Perplexity, ChatGPT, Claude) the structured Q&A format they preferentially extract for citations. LLMs love this format. The Eden currently has no FAQ surface anywhere on the site, which leaves a primary AEO/GEO lever inactive. An FAQ on the homepage and additional FAQ blocks on the rooms, Velvet Rose, and Coffee Manufactory pages would be quick to build and would compound visibility across both classical search and answer engines.

M-08
Minor Content strategy

Restaurant menus and key content live in PDFs, not HTML pages.

Three of the most engaging on-property experiences — Coffee Manufactory, Poolside Dining, Velvet Rose — link to PDF menus rather than HTML pages. PDFs do not rank, do not earn answer-engine citations, do not convert mobile users well, and create their own accessibility burden. Compounding this: the Coffee Manufactory and Poolside Dining cards on the homepage share copy verbatim.

Google Hotels metasearch is not surfacing the property's official website.

A historic property living a brand-new account's life.

Findings cover the property's owned social presence, audience scale, content cadence, and the gap between the brand's heritage and the surface area where that heritage should be visible.

S-01
Critical Audience scale

1,845 Instagram followers for a Santa Monica historic landmark with celebrity provenance.

The Eden's Instagram (@the.eden.sm) holds 1,845 followers across 109 posts. For context: a property reopened from Sonder/Beacon ownership in June 2025 will reasonably take time to rebuild — but the gap between the property's actual story (William E. S-02
Critical Site integration

The website's social section lists channels by name with no working links.

In the homepage footer: "FOLLOW · Facebook · Tripadvisor · X-twitter · Youtube · Instagram" — rendered as plain text with no detected outbound URLs in the source HTML. A guest who wants to follow the property cannot click to do so. An organic discovery moment is converted to friction. This is also a structured-data and link-equity miss (social profile links are part of the standard Organization schema's sameAs array, which Eden's current schema lacks).

S-03
Major Content strategy

No detected branded hashtag, UGC strategy, or content pillar discipline.

There is no consistent branded hashtag tying user-generated content to the property (the obvious candidates — #StayAtEden, #TheEdenSM, #EdenSantaMonica — are uncommitted in source observation). There is no detected UGC repost cadence, no creator-collaboration pattern, no thematic content pillars (heritage, design, dining, wellness, neighborhood) being executed against. The property has the raw material — 1939 architecture in a Streamline Moderne landmark, ocean views, a poolside cabana program, vinyl-and-cocktails programming at Velvet Rose, the Coffee Manufactory partnership, dog-friendly amenities, an active wedding and events business — and almost none of it is being staged into repeating content formats.

S-04
Major Content format

The feed is image-led; Instagram now prioritizes video and sound.

Instagram's distribution algorithm is now explicitly prioritizing Reels and short-form video as it competes with TikTok — a static-led feed simply does not reach the audience it would have two years ago. The Eden's account needs more Reels, more sound, and more video-led narrative. Specifically: more video collaborations, more UGC video reposts, and more "teach-me-something-unique" formats with text overlay to catch attention in the first second of a scroll. The text-overlay signal in particular is doing real work right now in algorithmic distribution — viewers who pause for a hook in the first frame are exactly the engagement the algorithm rewards.

S-05
Major Distribution channels

No detected TikTok presence; YouTube and X listed but unproven.

The footer claims Facebook, Tripadvisor, X (Twitter), YouTube, and Instagram. Search and discovery surfaces verify only the Instagram presence at meaningful audience. A property at this rate point and design caliber, opened post-2024, has a clear opening on TikTok where boutique hospitality content is one of the most-engaged verticals — Streamline Moderne architecture, ocean-view tours, vinyl-night Velvet Rose Reels, Coffee Manufactory pour shots all map to native TikTok content formats. Practically, the same Reels content built for Instagram should eventually cross-post to TikTok with format-appropriate adjustments — letting one production pass serve two platforms.

S-06

Minimal creator partnerships visible across Instagram, TikTok, or earned coverage.

Discovery search for hashtag mentions, geo-tagged content, and UGC at 1301 Ocean Avenue and at @the.eden.sm surfaces minimal third-party creator activity. Doubled Rook has spoken with several creators in our network who would welcome the opportunity to collaborate with The Eden, and a tiered program scoped against the property's Streamline Moderne heritage, Velvet Rose rooftop, Coffee Manufactory, and pet-friendly positioning would compound visibility quickly. Reference accounts like @hotelsabovepar and the editorial orbit around Architectural Digest are exactly the surface where this property's narrative belongs.

Chapter 05 — ADA Litigation Risk

Quantifying the likelihood of a demand letter or filed claim.

The Eden's exposure is scored across six factors: jurisdiction, vertical, scanner-visible defects, accessibility posture, monetary attractiveness, and remediation defenses. Each factor is rated 0–10; the composite is weighted toward the factors that most predict actual filings.

Context
California leads the United States in ADA Title III website filings, accounting for roughly a third of all such lawsuits in recent years. The state's Unruh Civil Rights Act adds $4,000 in statutory damages per violation, on top of any federal ADA exposure — making California hospitality properties uniquely attractive to plaintiff firms running automated scanner-driven sourcing.

Plain-English Read
California's serial-plaintiff ecosystem operates on a continuous basis: firms run automated scanners against hospitality and retail websites and source demand letters at volume. The exposure is active, not pending. A property matching The Eden's scanner signature in California can be contacted within weeks once it enters a plaintiff firm's queue — the question is not whether this profile gets sourced, but when. The realistic remediation cost for the homepage findings is materially smaller than a single Unruh Act settlement, typically a small fraction of the typical settlement-and-fees range cited for California Title III website cases.

Chapter 06 — Recommended Remediation

Sequenced to defuse risk first, then unlock revenue.

Phase order is intentional. Compliance and OTA cleanup remove the easiest plaintiff targets and stop the most acute revenue leak. The measurement and SEO foundations begin producing data the conversion engine then acts on.

A note on this section
This roadmap describes phases and outcomes — not the tactical implementation specifications. The fix-level work, named owners, and project plan are part of the engagement deliverable.
Phase 1 · Defuse Litigation Risk & OTA Bleeding
Week 1

Stop the active leaks.

  • Remediate the scanner-visible WCAG defects — alt text, headings, ARIA, links, accessibility statement, hero-video controls.
  • Submit URL and brand-correction requests to Booking.com and Hotels.com — preserve the 582-review equity, retire the legacy "sonder-the-beacon" slugs, fully transition descriptions and imagery to the current brand.
  • Verify Google Business Profile and Google Hotel Center — confirm "Matched" status, complete listing fields, ensure category is set to "Hotel," rebuild metasearch connectivity through Synxis.
  • Manually verify each major OTA listing — Booking, Expedia, Hotels.com, Agoda, Trip.com — to inventory any surface still carrying prior-brand language, photography, or amenity copy.
  • Re-categorize Tripadvisor from B&B / Inn to Hotel; update star class.
  • Reconcile the property's canonical phone number across the site, Google Business Profile, Visit Santa Monica, Yelp, Tripadvisor, and the long tail.
Phase 2 · Stand Up Measurement & Distribution Foundations
Weeks 2–4

Make the property legible to its own data and to search.

  • Connect the analytics stack that already exists — finish the GTM4WP / GA4 setup, add Meta Pixel and Microsoft Clarity, wire Synxis booking events.
  • Deploy a CMP with consent-mode-aware tracking; add CCPA "Do Not Sell or Share" footer link.
  • Title tags, meta descriptions, FAQ blocks, and LodgingBusiness schema across every page; AEO/GEO content scoped against the high-volume Santa Monica hotel keywords.
  • OTA photography refresh — bring Booking, Expedia, Tripadvisor, Yelp to 40+ professional photos with explicit categorization.
  • Performance pass — defer scripts, convert imagery to WebP, lazy-load below the fold, fix the broken mobile gallery, target mobile LCP < 2.5s to clear Google's Core Web Vitals threshold.
Phase 3 · Direct Booking, Content, & Social Engine
Weeks 4–10

Convert the audience the foundation now attracts.

  • Above-the-fold direct-book incentive; rate-parity language; pre-arrival email capture with welcome offer; email signup on every page.
  • Velvet Rose dedicated landing page with editorial hero, distinct photography, and structured data; F&B venues converted to HTML pages with menus migrated out of PDF.
  • Niche landing pages — Santa Monica weddings, ocean-view suites, wellness escapes, pet-friendly, ResortPass day-pass.
  • Booking-engine upsells — surface new deals and seasonal offers in Synxis tied to the social calendar and holiday cadence.
  • Social architecture consolidation — anchor master account, brand hashtag, content pillar discipline, Reels-with-sound cadence, TikTok presence stood up with cross-posting.
  • Influencer match and program — vetted creator slate matched to the property's Streamline Moderne heritage, Velvet Rose, Coffee Manufactory, and pet-friendly positioning.
  • Review-request automation — post-stay flow seeded into Synxis to scale review velocity across Tripadvisor, Google, and Booking.
  • Link-building via low-cost blog partnerships — local lifestyle, design, and travel publications tied to the heritage and neighborhood positioning.
Phase 4 · Sustained Performance & Optimization
Month 3+

Hold the gains. Compound them.

  • Monthly accessibility regression scan with a documented remediation log (a defensible posture in any future demand letter).
  • CRO program on the booking funnel, hero, and rate-display.
  • AEO / GEO content cadence — venue, neighborhood, and travel-intent answer pages aligned to the heritage and wellness positioning.
  • Quarterly performance review with the property team against direct-mix and RevPAR targets.
Adjacent · On-Property Activation

Where the digital strategy meets the property itself.

A handful of on-premise activations sit outside the digital scope but compound everything in the digital plan. They are inexpensive, fast to implement, and they feed the data and audience layer that the rest of the work depends on.

  • Front-desk email capture — every check-in, every check-out, woven into the standard greeting flow.
  • WiFi-gated email capture via a captive-portal solution (Nutshell or comparable) — guest gets free WiFi, property gets the email and segmentation data.
  • Loyalty program — even a lightweight stamp-card or tier system gives guests a reason to book direct on the next stay.
  • Low-cost brand activations — hotel-sponsored collaborations, rooftop movie nights at Velvet Rose, vinyl-night programming, neighborhood partnerships. These produce content at the same time they produce revenue.
A Note on Engagement Structure

Building the partnership in phases, proving viability before scaling.

During our last call I mentioned that the Foundations Tier paired with the Social Content Package would be a strong fit for the property. That bundle covers thirty assets per month for use across Eden's social channels, but does not include scheduling and posting, and does not cover PR or event planning.

After some discussion with my team, our recommendation is that the property would be better served by a dedicated content creator on the ground. I have someone on my team who lives near Santa Monica and would be able to handle content creation three times a week — shooting, editing, and producing the kind of video-led, sound-on, narrative content that Instagram and TikTok now reward. That option falls under our Premium Tier.

Longer term, we believe it would make sense for Eden's team to hire a dedicated social media manager in-house. We understand that additional headcount may not be the investment Eden is currently looking to make — which is why phasing this partnership makes the most sense:

Phase 1 · Fix the foundations

Foundations Tier — fix what's broken on the website, OTAs, and measurement. There's a real volume of work here, but most of it can move quickly. We strongly believe this phase yields an immediate boost to bookings and a favorable shift from OTA commissions to direct bookings.

Phase 2 · Scale once results show

As soon as the foundation work starts producing results, upgrading to the Premium Tier brings our on-the-ground content creator into the rotation — shooting and posting content 3x a week, executing the influencer program, and amplifying everything through Doubled Rook's distribution channels (paid ads, website, OTAs, on-premise strategy).

The property has what it takes to operate a tier above where it currently sits. Our team can match and brief influencers, assist with PR and events, and produce the content that scales the property's narrative. A few creators in our network have already expressed interest in collaborating with The Eden.

You mentioned wanting a partner who would work through ideas with you. That's how we work. The goal here is quality content, not quantity — which is why the playbook for The Eden looks different than the standard package. We build it together.

STR Performance Analysis:
Winning on index, losing on trend.

Stay three moves ahead of your comp set.
The one-line story. The property posted year-over-year RevPAR growth in all five months and never had a bad month in absolute terms — yet its RevPAR index (RGI) fell every single month, from 116.3 in January to 96.3 in May. Twenty points of market share evaporated in five months, and in May the property dropped below fair share on occupancy, rate, and RevPAR simultaneously for the first time in the dataset. Absolute growth is masking relative decline.
Five patterns, one direction.
1
An uninterrupted five-month RGI slide: 116.3 → 107.2 → 107.1 → 100.5 → 96.3
The property never had a bad month in absolute terms — RevPAR grew YoY all five months. What changed is the comp set: set occupancy grew +18.7%, +14.4%, +8.6%, +28.0%, +14.5% on essentially flat supply. This is comp-set demand recovery, and no counter-move has landed. Twenty points of RGI evaporated in five months.
2
The group base is the structural hole
Group MPI: 113.5 (Jan) → 56.6 → 55.8 → n/a → 53.3. Since February, comp hotels run roughly double the property's group mix (May: 20.0% vs. 10.7%). When group does book, it prices well — group ARI 103–110 in March and May. The problem is volume, not rate. That is a sales and citywide-alignment problem more than a marketing one, and it caps everything downstream.
3
Transient is the franchise
Transient MPI never dropped below 118 and hit 164 in February. The transient demand engine — leisure positioning, digital, direct channels — works. But transient alone cannot fill the Tuesday–Wednesday troughs a group base would.
4
Chronic ARI below 100, concentrated Sunday–Wednesday
ARI by month: 99.4, 93.9, 97.4, ~97.9, 97.5. The property fills more rooms than the set at a persistent 2–6% rate discount. The gap concentrates early-week, and the buy-occupancy-with-rate pattern worsened in April (Monday ADR −21.6%, Tuesday −14.8%).
5
The weekday/weekend inversion spread
January–February: winning weekdays, leaking weekends (Feb weekend RGI 99.2, Saturday MPI −16.8%). By May, both sides were below fair share — weekday RGI 94.1, weekend RGI 97.3. The erosion is no longer confined to one part of the week.

The official DMO listing reads "this year's $40-million dollar renovation," and describes Sweet Sleeper beds, 27" flat-screen TVs, and complimentary HBO — copy that predates the current renovation by well over a decade. Visit Anaheim is the first stop for citywide convention planners overflowing the Anaheim Convention Center. (Note: the listing's "$40M" figure also conflicts with the renovation investment as we understand it — worth confirming the correct number before republishing.)
Major · Reputation
Tripadvisor is the outlier dragging the planner-diligence story
3.0 on Tripadvisor vs 8.8 on Booking.com and 9.0 on Hotels.com. The gap isn't product — it's review velocity. ~247 lifetime Tripadvisor reviews on a 490-room hotel means legacy pre-renovation reviews still dominate the average. Meeting planners, wedding couples, and team managers routinely gut-check Tripadvisor before signing; a 3.0 next to a Westin or Marriott flag at 4+ is a silent RFP killer. Management response discipline is already good — what's missing is a systematic post-stay and post-event review-generation program aimed specifically at this channel.
Major · Corporate Channel
LinkedIn — the corporate planner's native habitat — is effectively dark
223 followers, no content cadence, a bare "National Sales" tagline, and a website link to sheratonparkanaheim.com, which redirects straight to Marriott.com. There is no property-owned narrative for the exact audience that books corporate meetings, training seminars, and retreats. Industry-wide, company-page organic reach has collapsed; the play is a light company-page refresh plus people-first publishing from the DOSM and sales managers' personal profiles, where reach and reply rates are dramatically higher.
Major · Weddings
Wedding marketplaces are selling the pre-renovation ballroom
The Knot and WeddingWire listings still describe the "Park Plaza Ballroom" at 600 guests; the renovated Park Ballroom seats 680 banquet / 1,100 theater. Review sentiment is outstanding (4.6, 92% recommend) but thin at 15 reviews, and the photography narrative doesn't lead with the post-renovation product or the property's genuinely unique sales hook — Disneyland fireworks as the reception backdrop.
Minor · Consistency
No two channels agree on the basic facts
Square footage appears as 34,892 / ~30,000 / 26,274 / 26,000 / 11,035 depending on channel. Older intermediaries (e.g., HotelPlanner) still cite 486 rooms, a "$9 renovation in 2014," and Morton's-era F&B. Ballroom naming varies. Beyond confusing human planners, this inconsistency now propagates into AI answer engines (ChatGPT, Gemini, Claude), which synthesize these sources when a planner asks "which Anaheim hotels can host 500 attendees?" — an AEO/GEO problem that compounds every year it isn't fixed.
Positive · Keep Doing
The foundations worth building on
Marriott.com's events hub is complete and well-structured (full capacity chart, floor plans, eRFP). Wedding reviews that do exist are glowing and the team responds within 24 hours. Management answers Tripadvisor reviews consistently. Leisure OTA scores prove post-reno sentiment is excellent wherever review volume is healthy. Engine's corporate listing is rich and current. The raw material for a strong group story is all here.

The Compounding Effect

These segments share one infrastructure: accurate channel data, current photography, review velocity, and property-owned landing pages for niche demand. Fix the foundation once and every segment inherits it — then each segment needs only its own content layer and outreach motion on top.

Who Is Actually Booking — SMERF & Athletic

SMERF
Social · Military · Educational · Religious · Fraternal
  • Religious — the hotel is already on the inside. RECongress (Feb 20–22, 2026, plus Youth Day), the largest annual Catholic gathering in the U.S. at ~12,000 post-COVID attendees, lists 1855 S. Harbor Blvd in its official housing block at $218–263. That's proof of relationship. The upside is converting one-weekend block status into year-round faith-market preference: diocesan retreats, parish jubilees, and the Spanish- and Vietnamese-language ministry communities RECongress itself programs for — the same OC communities driving the cultural-wedding opportunity.
  • Educational — Disney is running a student-group machine next door. Disney Imagination Campus brings school bands, choirs, dance and theater programs to perform inside the parks year-round (minimums: 20 stage performers / 50 marching band; groups book 10+ rooms), amplified in 2026 by the Disneyland 70th "Celebrate Happy" push and Grad Nite season each May–June. Critically, the booking door is the student-tour-operator layer — Music Travel Consultants, Performing Arts Consultants, Educational Travel Adventures, and peers who package "on or off property" hotels. Being the preferred off-property hotel for three or four of these operators is a room-night annuity.
  • Military & fraternal: military reunion groups (ship, squadron, unit associations) pick destination cities 1–2 years out through reunion planners and value published per-diem-friendly rates, hospitality-suite space, and banquet capacity — all of which the hotel has and none of which it publishes. Fraternal and service-org district conventions (Elks, Rotary, Lions, Greek-letter conclaves) buy the same package. This segment's date flexibility is the natural fill for need periods — including the Nov–Jan window the hotel's own Cvent need-dates already advertise.
Athletic & Entertainment
Traveling Sports Teams · Music Tour Crews · Performance Troupes
  • Cheer and dance nationals own the Convention Center for weeks. USA Spirit Nationals & Collegiate Championships (Feb 19–23, 2026) and USA All Star Cheer & Dance Super Nationals (Mar 13–15, 2026 — 7,000+ athletes, 15,000 spectators), plus JAMZ nationals, land thousands of teams a block from the hotel. These families book through event housing partners and open-market search; team moms are decision-makers, and they decide months out.

That is a named, addressable gap.

A one-page crew fact sheet and presence in crew-travel databases outworks any ad campaign here.

Paid, Website, Social: What Fits and What Doesn't

Paid Media
Where Dollars Actually Reach Group Deciders
  • Google Search, non-brand, intent-led: always-on campaigns against "meeting space near Anaheim Convention Center," "Anaheim wedding venues," "hotel room blocks Anaheim," "team hotel near Disneyland," with ad copy and landing pages per segment. Volume is modest but intent is surgical — this is RFP acquisition, not room-night arbitrage. Layer event-calendar flighting: budgets pulse 6–10 months ahead of citywides and 3–5 months ahead of cheer/volleyball weekends, matching each segment's booking window.
  • Meta (Facebook/Instagram) for the visual segments: weddings (engaged-audience targeting, fireworks-reception video creative, cultural-wedding creative variants in-language), family reunions (interest + lookalike), and tournament families (radius flighting around ACC/ASC event weekends). Creative is included in scope, so testing variants costs iteration, not invoices.
  • Cvent advertising: the one paid channel corporate planners actually source in — priority placement timed to citywide sourcing windows (carried forward from the channel audit's finding that competitor slots currently sit on the hotel's own profile).
  • What we'd skip: No Fit paid LinkedIn (CPMs are punishing for a single-property meetings message; organic people-first from the sales team outperforms it), metasearch for group demand (Google Hotel Ads and OTA metasearch are transient-booking rails — group RFPs don't flow through them), and any runDisney-pegged spend during the hiatus. Brand-standard note: all property-level paid runs inside Marriott franchise digital guidelines — vanity domains, brand-term bidding rules, and approved-agency requirements are respected in campaign design, not discovered after launch.
Website
Owned Ground the Hotel Currently Doesn't Have
  • Stand up the groups vanity site. sheratonparkanaheim.com currently just redirects to Marriott.com — there is no property-owned page for any group segment. A compliant vanity microsite with five landing pages (Meetings & Conventions · Weddings & Celebrations · Sports Teams · Reunions & SMERF · Tour & Crew Logistics) becomes the destination every ad, post, and sales email points to — each with segment-specific proof (capacity chart, fireworks gallery, two-queen inventory, bus parking, per-diem-friendly rates) and an RFP form routed straight to sales.
  • Make it the single source of truth. Publish the canonical fact sheet (34,892 sq ft, 17 rooms, correct ballroom naming) with EventVenue/Hotel schema markup — the AEO/GEO fix that teaches Google and AI answer engines the right numbers, counteracting the conflicting data currently syndicated across Expedia, Cvent, and the CVB (prior audit).
  • Measurement-first: RFP-form and call tracking, GA4 events per segment page, and source attribution on every lead — so in 90 days the hotel knows which segment pages produce RFPs, not just visits.
  • What we'd skip: No Fit a blog-content program chasing generic "things to do in Anaheim" SEO — Disney and Visit Anaheim own that shelf; the microsite wins on specificity, not volume.
Social Media
Two Pillars Added, One Channel Consciously Skipped
  • Instagram/Facebook — add a celebrations pillar. Current feeds are 100% leisure. Add wedding and event content (ballroom transformation time-lapses, fireworks-backdrop receptions, cultural-wedding features, "team welcome" tournament-weekend posts). This feeds the Meta paid engine and converts the wedding-marketplace traffic that already checks Instagram before inquiring.
  • LinkedIn — organic, people-first. DOSM and sales-manager profiles publishing venue walkthroughs, citywide-overflow availability notes, and post-event case snippets; company page refreshed as the anchor (fixing the dead vanity-URL link, per the audit). Person-to-person reach on LinkedIn dramatically outperforms company-page posting — this costs cadence, not budget.
  • YouTube as an RFP-support library, not a channel to grow: one venue walkthrough, one ballroom-flip, one crew-logistics tour — embedded on landing pages and linked in sales follow-ups.
  • What we'd skip: No Fit TikTok (no group decision-maker density; a leisure play the flag's channels already cover) and follower-count campaigns of any kind — group social is judged on RFPs influenced, not audience size.
